When someone answers

I'm looking to gain some clinical experience this summer through physician shadowing or volunteering. Is there someone I could speak with about whether your practice might be open to that?"
When connected to the office manager or physician
"Hi, I'm Rishi. I'm a high school senior applying to combined BS/MD medical programs, and I have two years of biomedical coursework from my school's health sciences academy. I'm very motivated and genuinely passionate about medicine. I was hoping to shadow a physician for a few hours a week this summer — even 4–6 hours weekly would be incredibly valuable for me. I'd be respectful of your schedule and happy to help the team in any way. Would that be something you might consider?"
If they say YES
"Thank you so much — I really appreciate it. Could I get your email so I can send a brief note with my background? I want to make it as easy as possible for you."

"I completely understand. Would it be okay if I sent a short email with my background, just in case anything opens up? I'm very flexible on timing."
→ Always leave the door open. A no today can become a yes in 2 weeks.

Essay Framework

Common App Personal Statement · 650 words · built around Rishi's real story

The one sentence that holds the whole essay together
"I've spent my life learning how to keep people safe — now I want to learn how to heal them."
Everything Rishi has done connects to this. Lifeguarding is literally about keeping people alive. Boy Scouts is about preparedness and service. Swimming to States is about discipline under pressure. Robotics is applying technology to solve problems. MATA and HOSA are the science behind those lives. The essay's job is to show how all of these are chapters of the same story — not a list of achievements, but a journey toward medicine.

📐 Paragraph-by-Paragraph Structure
1
The Hook — Open on the Pool Deck
~80 words
What it does
Drop the reader into a specific moment on the pool deck. Don't explain it yet — just put them there. The best essays open in the middle of a scene, not with "I have always wanted to be a doctor."
Write about one specific moment as a lifeguard — a moment of tension, a close call, or a quiet moment where the weight of responsibility hit you. What did you see? What were you thinking? What did you do?
Sensory, present tense, specific. The reader should feel the heat, hear the noise, see what Rishi sees when he scans the water.
2
Zoom Out — The Bigger Pattern
~100 words
What it does
Step back and show the pattern started before the pool deck. Boy Scouts, swimming, the lifeguard certification — same instinct, different chapters.
Touch briefly on Boy Scouts and swimming — not as achievements but as earlier chapters of the same story. What did they teach you? What do they share with lifeguarding?
Don't list. Connect. One or two sentences each, then land back on the pool deck.
3
The Turn — From Safe to Healed
~120 words
The most important paragraph
This is the pivot. Something changed the question from "how do I keep this person safe?" to "how do I understand what's happening inside them?" This is where lifeguarding meets medicine.
Was there a moment where you wished you knew more — what was happening inside someone's body, not just what to do on the outside? Or a MATA moment that clicked with something from the pool? That intersection is the heart of this essay.
This should feel like a light turning on. The shift from "I keep people safe" to "I want to understand why bodies fail and how to fix them" is the emotional core of the whole piece.
4
MATA & HOSA — Where Safety Became Science
~120 words
What it does
Bring in the biomedical program — not as a résumé item but as the place where the curiosity sparked by lifeguarding found a home. What specific thing from MATA changed how he sees things?

✅ Do's and Don'ts
✓ Do
→Open with a specific scene, not a general statement about medicine
→Name real things — an actual MATA topic, a real pool moment, a specific Scout experience
→Let accomplishments show up through the story, not as a list
→Sound like a thoughtful 17-year-old — not a brochure
→Circle back to the opening image in the closing paragraph
→Write the first draft badly — just get it out, then refine
✗ Don't
→Start with "I have always wanted to be a doctor" or "Ever since I was young..."
→List accomplishments — it reads like a résumé
→Use "passion," "journey," "unique," "driven," or "make a difference"
→Explain everything — trust the reader to make connections
→Write a conclusion that summarizes what the reader just read
→Try to be impressive — try to be honest

On Writing Well
William Zinsser · Book · ~$12
The single best book on clear, honest nonfiction writing. Chapter 7 covers science writing specifically. Read chapters 1–5 before starting the first draft — Zinsser's core ideas about clarity and cutting clutter will immediately improve Rishi's sentences.

Bird by Bird
Anne Lamott · Book · ~$14
Famous for the concept of the "shitty first draft" — giving permission to write badly at first and fix it later. Directly addresses the paralysis most students feel staring at a blank page. Read chapters 1 and 2 before Rishi's first draft session.
💡 For Rishi: The lesson is simple — just start writing. The first draft isn't supposed to be good. It's supposed to exist. Once it exists, it can be fixed. A blank page cannot.

The Elements of Style
Strunk & White · Free online · Also ~$8 as book
Tiny book, enormous impact. Read it in one sitting. The rules — omit needless words, use active voice, prefer the specific over the general — are exactly what makes college essays work.
💡 For Rishi: Keep the "Elementary Principles of Composition" page open while revising. Run every sentence through rule 13: "Omit needless words." Most first drafts are 20% longer than they need to be.

Politics and the English Language
George Orwell · Free Online · 20-minute read
Six rules for writing clearly that directly apply to college essays. Written in 1946 but every rule holds. Especially rule 1: never use a long word where a short one will do. Most college essays fail because they try to sound impressive instead of clear.
💡 For Rishi: Print out the six rules and keep them next to his laptop while writing. Rule 6 is the most important: "Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous." It's permission to trust your instincts.
